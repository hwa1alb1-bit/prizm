# Product

## Register

product

## Users

PRIZM is for firm operators: accountants, bookkeepers, and small-firm owners who convert client bank statements into ledger-ready output. They handle sensitive financial documents, work under client deadlines, and need proof that files, exports, billing, and account access are controlled.

Daily users may include staff who upload statements, review extracted rows, reconcile issues, export results, and answer client questions. Buyer users care about accuracy, privacy, retention, auditability, and whether the product reduces risk without slowing the firm down.

## Product Purpose

PRIZM is a trust-first, SOC 2-ready bank statement conversion workflow. Its job is to turn uploaded bank statements into verified ledger data while making the security model visible enough for a professional user to trust it.

The product should describe trust claims as precise commitments tied to implementation state. When PRIZM says files auto-delete in 24 hours, audit events are written, workspace isolation exists, or errors are traceable, the interface should show evidence through status, receipts, timestamps, audit history, clear labels, and documented controls.

Success means a firm can upload a statement, understand what is happening, resolve exceptions, export usable output, and prove what happened later.

## Brand Personality

Precise. Calm. Evidence-led.

PRIZM should feel like a premium workflow product for financial operations, closer to Stripe or Linear in discipline than generic accounting software. The voice is plainspoken and exact. It avoids hype, legal fog, and decorative confidence. It should make hard operational promises feel understandable, inspectable, and boring in the best way.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic SaaS gloss: vague trust copy, hero metrics, identical card grids, decorative security badges, gradient theater, and claims that sound confident without showing evidence.

Avoid policy-only compliance UX. PRIZM should not bury trust in documents while the workflow itself stays opaque.

Avoid consumer fintech flash: neon dashboards, gamified money visuals, crypto-adjacent styling, and animation that makes sensitive financial work feel unserious.

Avoid slow enterprise compliance portals. Controls should be visible, but they should not make common accounting workflows feel heavy.

## Design Principles

1. Evidence over assertion.
   Every trust claim needs a surface-level proof point. Use status, receipts, timestamps, retention windows, audit events, connector health, trace IDs, and documented controls.

2. Keep accounting in flow.
   Upload, review, exception handling, reconciliation, export, billing, and API-key work should stay calm and direct. The product should reduce cognitive load during repetitive firm operations.

3. Make compliance operational.
   SOC 2 readiness, 24-hour deletion, workspace isolation, rate limits, and error schemas are not marketing decorations. They are product behaviors that should be visible at the right moment.

4. Name every state clearly.
   Users should never wonder whether a document is uploading, processing, waiting on OCR, ready for review, exported, expiring, deleted, failed, or blocked by a billing or permission issue.

5. Respect workspace boundaries.
   Multi-tenant context, roles, ownership, and audit history should be clear enough to prevent mistakes without adding noise to single-workspace Phase 1 flows.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Use WCAG 2.2 AA as the baseline. Support keyboard navigation, visible focus, reduced motion, color-blind-safe status states, readable density, and clear error recovery.

Do not communicate important states by color alone. Upload, OCR, deletion, export, billing, and permission states need text labels or icons with accessible names.

Write for professionals under time pressure. Prefer direct language, short sentences, and concrete remediation over vague reassurance.
